Fort Ben App

Mobile Design & Testing

  • Goal: Design an AR app about the history and culture of Fort Benjamin Harrison
  • Clients: Schneider Geospatial and Arts for Lawrence
  • Users: Residents of and visitors to the Fort Ben area
  • My Role: outreach, requirements analysis, competitive research, user research, report writing, user and task analysis, feature ideation, storyboarding, prototyping (XD, Unity), test protocol development, test facilitation, test data analysis, editing, presentation

Arts for Lawrence won a $5.8M endowment to promote the history and culture of the former Fort Benjamin Harrison military base. They hired Schneider Geospatial to develop a history-focused augmented reality mobile app. Schneider needed a design to guide their development. They agreed to let our IUPUI capstone team help.

We researched existing historical AR systems. We surveyed local historical groups. We arranged three tours from Fort Ben history experts. Our resulting client presentation included a survey analysis, personas for residents and visitors, journey maps, and feature suggestions.

With our clients we selected two features:

  • A historical “Day in the Life” tour with a virtual guide.
  • A timeline of historical images of the Theater, to align with its current appearance.

We created storyboards and low-fidelity prototypes to discuss with the clients. We experimented with technologies for a higher-fidelity prototype before selecting Principle. We conducted six user test sessions outdoors in unexpectedly wintry conditions. We compiled our findings and recommendations into a report.

The image alignment feature (shown above) had design issues, but testers loved the idea. Testers loved the virtual tour guide. Our capstone presentation won a People’s Choice award. Schneider will begin development on the app later in 2020. We learned to make contingency plans when user testing on-location outdoors.

Telepathic

Game Design

  • Goal: Create an elegant cooperative tabletop game
  • Clients: A personal passion for cooperative games
  • Users: People who want to play with a partner instead of against them
  • My Role: Game design, prototyping, user testing, visual design (Illustrator, InDesign), rules writing, collaboration, publishing

I wanted to design a cooperative game to play with my spouse. I started with colored markers on index cards and a simple set of rules:

  1. Two players must arrange tiles to match both their win conditions.
  2. Neither player knows the other’s win condition.
  3. The players cannot speak.

Initial playtests were encouraging. A friend volunteered the artwork. I designed the components and box in Adobe Illustrator. After playtests indicated the design was solid, I wrote the rulebook, laid it out in InDesign, and conducted blind playtests to validate it. I set up a shop page on The Game Crafter. Friends helped me create a promo video. By product launch I had tested the prototype with over 80 players, from family and friends to expert game designers and publishers.

For a Serious Games class in Fall 2019, I conducted tests comparing the experience of playing Telepathic face-to-face versus playing with a remote partner over the Internet. What I learned helped me to improve the manual and component design, and to start creating a digital prototype.

My spouse doesn’t care for the game, but my crowd sale sold over 100 copies.

Graspable Math

Education Tech

  • Goal: Improve user onboarding and retention
  • Client: Graspable Math
  • Users: Math educators
  • My Role: competitive analysis, cognitive walkthroughs, Google analytics, interviewing, surveying, affinity diagramming, concept mapping, story mapping, wireframing (Adobe XD)

Graspable Math provides an online tactile interface for solving algebra equations (see above). Their site had good traffic, but poor user retention. As a UX intern they asked me to improve the onboarding experience.

I documented cognitive walkthroughs of competing products. I calculated user churn from Google analytics data. I attended training sessions, surveyed target users, and interviewed expert users. I documented critical usability issues, such as:

  • The design worked poorly on small screens, despite the reliance upon tablets and mobile devices in classrooms.
  • Personal classroom materials could not be organized.
  • Public classroom materials were undiscoverable.

To discuss how to address the issues, I created a concept map and a story map. I created a wireframe prototype in Adobe XD which overhauled the main interactive canvas (see below, with menu open) and provided materials organization. I conducted four online usability tests to validate the prototype.

My reports offered Graspable Math many suggestions for improving their onboarding experience. They hired a UX designer as I was leaving, whom I hope will carry on the work that I started. I was grateful for the opportunity to do professional UX work.

Healthcare Portal Study

Portal Redesign

  • Goal: Design a more usable healthcare portal
  • Context: Interaction Design Practice class, IUPUI
  • Users: Healthcare patients and providers
  • My Role: interviewing, competitive analysis, affinity diagramming, concept mapping, storyboarding, prototyping (Adobe XD), expert evaluation

Healthcare portals connect patients and providers via the Internet. They provide an alternative to time-constrained and time-consuming communication by phone. To research them, we gathered information from the following sources:

  • Two healthcare portals
  • Five providers (a physician assistant, two dentists, a doctor, and a pharmacist)
  • One patient who manages 20 prescriptions for herself and her spouse

We discovered that portals are not realizing their potential:

  • Portal vendors focus on providing features instead of usability.
  • Portals often use medical jargon instead of information patients can understand.
  • Liability and regulations constrain portal use.
  • Portals lack the personal connection that patients feel with voice communication.

Based on our research we designed a prescription management subsystem for a healthcare portal. We selected this area because it is a common and important interaction involving multiple parties (patient, caregiver, physician, and pharmacist) in a workflow. We created storyboards, whiteboarded screens, and created a prototype with Adobe XD. Four HCI experts and a physician assistant evaluated the prototype, and provided much helpful input.

Much work would remain to complete the prescription subsystem. We learned some things about the needs of elderly users and the requirements of healthcare systems. We also gained practice with Adobe XD and creating validation scenarios.

Improving Online Gaming

Research, observation, interviewing, affinity diagramming, brainstorming, storyboarding, heuristic evaluation, think-aloud evaluation

  • Context: Interaction Design Practice class, IUPUI
  • Goal: Address social issues in online gaming
  • Users: Players of online games, especially “noobs”

Online games connect millions of people. Healthy social connections are a key predictor of happiness and longevity. But toxic online interactions can lead to depression, anxiety, physical violence, and suicide. How can we help gamers find good people to play with, and also mitigate toxic behavior?

We researched studies of player toxicity and watched videos of player behavior. We observed players at a campus gaming center and a public eSports facility. And we interviewed six gamers about their experiences. We learned the following:

  • Most toxicity doesn’t come from a few bad apples. It erupts from almost everyone in the heat of battle, especially when some noob costs them a win.
  • Most players don't report toxicity, because:
    • people disagree upon what actions are toxic,
    • reports are hard to substantiate, and
    • punishments seem ineffective.

In response we designed an authenticated player reputation hub. It uses a quick in-game reporting feature to:

  1. Immediately block communication with the offender,
  2. Prevent future matches with the offender, and
  3. Capture a transcript of game and communication activity for automated analysis, to determine if either player’s reputation should be affected.

We evaluated a paper prototype by expert review and three think-aloud tests. Our testers indicated that they would use the system if it could be implemented.

Nexus

Game design, competitive research, sketching, storyboarding, prototyping (Balsamiq), user testing

  • Context: Prototyping for Interactive Systems class, IUPUI
  • Goal: Design a game that is fun and beneficial
  • Users: Augmented Reality (AR) game players

We wanted to make a video game that encourages players to run around in the real world and work together with their friends. To that end, we designed a cooperative augmented reality game for smartphones.

Nexus is like a Ghostbusters tower-defense game you play in a park. Your team runs around to gather resources and build towers. The towers zap evil spirits that travel along ley lines toward your base. If too many spirits reach the base, you lose.

After some initial discussion, I researched some other AR games and wrote a game design document. For our first prototype we drew some storyboards and selected some basic tasks:

  • Forming a team
  • Communicating with teammates
  • Gathering resources
  • Building defenses

We tested the prototype in Balsamiq with three participants. From those results we made a higher fidelity Sketch prototype and an experience video. Our class enjoyed our presentation, and several people encouraged us to keep developing the game.

Outdoor Exhibit Survey

Wireframing, analytics, coding (JavaScript, D3, GitHub), client rapport, team leadership

  • Context: Visualization Design, Anlysis, & Evalutation class, IUPUI
  • Goal: Provide an interactive visualization of survey data
  • Users: Research team at a large local children's museum

The client had surveyed visitors leaving a new outdoor exhibit. They wanted to discover patterns or trends in the data. We sketched a visualization to investigate the following:

  • How did visitors respond to the exhibit, based upon:
    • Museum membership?
    • Age ranges in their group?
    • Where they came from?
    • Daily weather conditions?
  • What forms of outreach were most effective at drawing visitors of different demographics?

After the client approved the sketch, we cleaned and harmonized the data. I managed our GitHub repository, developed the line chart, and embedded all our charts into one page. I created the data filters and helped apply them to the bar charts. (They were not compatible with the maps.) And I demonstrated the visualization to the client.

This was a learning experience. The client used five different survey instruments, complicating the data. I tried to do too much with the line chart, and the results are not as illuminating as I had hoped. The age filters are confusing because they are not mutually exclusive like the other filters. (A visitor cannot be from Marion County and an Adjacent County, but they can have 0-5 year olds and 6-12 year olds in the same group.) But the client appreciated our effort, and it encouraged them to consider other interactive data visualizations in their future work.

Data Transfer

Interviewing, use case analysis, analytics, information architecture, user interface design, coding (C#, SQL, XML), client rapport

  • Context: Covance (a contract pharmaceutical research organization)
  • Goal: Generate data files from clinical trial results
  • Users: Covance data analysts (service contacts to company clients)

The data analysts could not meet customer demand for increasingly large and complex files. We rebuilt the Data Transfer 3 system with the following improvements:

  • A more flexible report design interface.
  • User-defined attributes and functions.
  • A browser to research change to datapoints over time.
  • Schedule-based processing.
  • Transactional processing, allowing transmission of small update files instead of re-sending the entire file.

Data Transfer 4 had better usability, performance, scalability, and compliance with FDA standards. By 2007 it was generating 20 gigabytes of data in 1400 reports every week.
