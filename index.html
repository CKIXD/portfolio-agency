Healthcare Portal Study

Interviewing, competitive analysis, affinity diagramming, concept mapping, storyboarding, prototyping (Adobe XD), expert evaluation

  • Context: Interaction Design Practice class, IUPUI
  • Goal: Design a more usable healthcare portal
  • Users: Healthcare patients and providers

Healthcare portals connect patients and providers via the Internet. They provide an alternative to time-constrained and time-consuming communication by phone. To research them, we gathered information from the following sources:

  • Two healthcare portals
  • Five providers (a physician assistant, two dentists, a doctor, and a pharmacist)
  • One patient who manages 20 prescriptions for herself and her spouse

We discovered that portals are not realizing their potential:

  • Portal vendors focus on providing features instead of usability.
  • Portals often deliver medical jargon instead of information patients can understand.
  • Liability and regulations constrain portal use.
  • Portals lack the personal connection that patients feel over voice communication.

From our research we designed an Adobe XD prototype of a prescription management portal subsystem. We selected this area because it is a common and important interaction involving multiple parties (patient, caregiver, physician, and pharmacist) in a workflow. We performed three rounds of evaluations with subject matter and HCI experts. Much work would remain to complete the design, but we made a good start and have clear direction on what to do next.

Outdoor Exhibit Survey

Wireframing, analytics, coding (JavaScript, D3, GitHub), client rapport, team leadership

  • Context: Visualization Design, Anlysis, & Evalutation class, IUPUI
  • Goal: Provide an interactive visualization of survey data
  • Users: Research team at The Children’s Museum of Indianapolis

The client had surveyed visitors leaving a new outdoor exhibit. They wanted to discover patterns or trends in the data. We sketched a visualization to investigate the following:

  • How did visitors respond to the exhibit, based upon:
    • Museum membership?
    • Age ranges in their group?
    • Where they came from?
    • Daily weather conditions?
  • What forms of outreach were most effective at drawing visitors of different demographics?

After the client approved the sketch, we cleaned and harmonized the data. I managed our GitHub repository, developed the line chart, and embedded all our charts into one page. I created the data filters and helped apply them to the bar charts. (They were not compatible with the maps.) And I demonstrated the visualization to the client.

This was a learning experience. The client used five different survey instruments, complicating the data. I tried to do too much with the line chart, and the results are not as illuminating as I had hoped. The age filters are confusing because they are not mutually exclusive like the other filters. (A visitor cannot be from Marion County and an Adjacent County, but they can have 0-5 year olds and 6-12 year olds in the same group.) But the client appreciated our effort, and it encouraged them to consider other interactive data visualizations in their future work.
